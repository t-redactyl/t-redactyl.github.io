Proof by mathematical induction: introduction

written January 04, 2020 in maths

As part of my study for my maths degree, I’m currently studying abstract mathematics, which involves learning how to prove all sorts of statements. One type of proof I really struggled with initially was proof by mathematical induction. However, after being recommended the wonderful Book of Proof by Richard Hammack, this method of proving statements finally clicked for me. As such I decided to create a few blog posts to hopefully help anyone who is similarly stuck (and help me remember myself!).

Motivating example

Let’s say that we wish to prove that the sum \(1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + \ldots + n\) is equal to \(\frac{n^2 + n}{2}\) for every positive integer \(n\). Where do we start? We could break down this statement for each case of \(n\) like so:

$S_n$ Sum of first $n$ positive numbers $\frac{n^2 + n}{2}$
$S_1$ 1 1
$S_2$ 1 + 2 3
$S_3$ 1 + 2 + 3 6
$S_4$ 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 10
$S_5$ 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 15

Let us say that we wish to prove that the sum \(a + ar + ar^2 + \ldots + ar^{n - 1}\) is equal to \(a\frac{(1 - r^n)}{1-r}\), where \(r \neq 1\) and \(n \in \mathbb{N}\). Oof, this is a lot. Let’s break it down.
